Rebuilding Deepdrive on Unreal Engine
April 8, 2018

TLDR; Deepdrive has been rebuilt without GTA and now includes support for multiple cameras, depth, and running natively on Linux - All open source on Unreal Engine. Check it out!

Background - or skip to Unreal stuff

A little over a year ago, I worked with OpenAI to release an integration for GTAV into the Universe platform. The goal was to make one of the most complex video games available to researchers to experiment with methods like deep reinforcement learning for self-driving.

However, it turned out that TakeTwo was not as receptive to people using GTAV for this purpose as I had hoped.

I apologize for not having gotten TakeTwo's permission to use GTA. I know that many of you had spent time with Deepdrive 1.0 and Deepdrive Universe, and am deeply sorry for not being able to continue to support you. I hope that rebuilding the project on Unreal will be able to restore what you found valuable in the GTA version, and eventually do much more.

Before Deepdrive Universe launched, I had started work at Uber's Advanced Technologies Group on their perception team. Deepdrive caught their eye as relevant experience applying deep learning to self-driving. It was also an opportunity to incorporate real world concerns back into Deepdrive, something I was very grateful Uber was supportive of.


Levine's Fall 2017 Deep RL course slides

One thing I noticed early on there was that I had solved the same problems in simulation as had been solved for real-world cars, but in a much different way. For example, in imitation learning, you often need to reduce sampling error when the training and test distributions are not IID.


Bojarski et al. ‘16, NVIDIA

In real cars, this is dealt with by "pretending" as if the left or right camera is in the center of the car and generating a corresponding corrective steering action to get back to the center of the lane. Viewpoint transformation then can interpolate between cameras to generate additional corrective labels under the assumption of an infinite horizon - i.e. flat roads.

In simulation, we can (safely!) vary the trajectory of the car and see what an oracle path-following AI does to correct its trajectory.


Using motion capture sensors for training a robot to act using just a camera. Levine, Finn, et al. 16

So it’d be great to use this simulated training to improve real-world performance as we can try things with zero safety risk. Not only that, but we can "cheat" and use the game's perfect 3D world model with techniques like this and this to adapt driving to the real-world.

Excitingly, we found that neural networks trained on real-world driving were able to drive in simulated environments, and therefore going the other direction was promising, although not something we pursued.

Over the course of a year there, I was able to familiarize myself more with the problems real self-driving cars ran into by digging into the fundamental reasons for our disengagements and riding in test cars. Doing so I became increasingly excited about what an open self-driving simulator could to help solve many of these problems.

So in November, I left to do just that and began work on Deepdrive 2.0.

The move to Unreal


Some of the popular games made in Unreal - Gears 4, PUBG, and Fortnite from left to right. Epic, the company behind Unreal Engine, also created Gears and Fortnite. PUBG and Fortnite are currently the most popular online games with 2M and 3M concurrent users respectively (that's close to the number of concurrent users on Facebook!).

We evaluated many engines and Unreal not only checked the boxes, but felt best to us. From a feature perspective Unreal is the only free, shared source, AAA engine available. Having the source is critical as we might need to do crazy stuff like optimize eight camera rendering, swapping out the physics engine, or running faster than real-time.

There are a lot of other engines out there, including some really interesting ones like Godot, however when it comes to realism - Unity, Unreal, CryEngine and are still quite far ahead of the pack.

Other open source projects like AirSim and Carla are also using Unreal. It’s my hope these sims can reuse components and cooperate. We have already worked with the Carla team on some synchronization functionality and it’s very exciting to see the growing community around open self-driving. In addition, the self-driving simulators at NVIDIA, Cruise, and Zoox are built on Unreal.

You may be wondering why we created our own sim in Unreal with all of these others out there. While we actually were not aware of Carla when we started or that AirSim was doing more than drones, we found the following out after trying these ourselves:

  1. Our frame rates are higher (link to FAQ) while capturing several cameras, due to use of shared memory rather than sockets and asynchronous transfer.
  2. Our road surface is not flat, but hilly, curvy, and with different widths at different points
  3. Our assets - the map, cars, lighting, etc… are free, come with the project, and modifiable in Unreal.

However, I think it’s a great thing to have a variety of simulators available. A cross-simulator benchmark would certainly be intriguing - as domain shift is a major obstacle for simulation in general. If you’re interested in working on this, let me know.

Here you see some of the settings available for changing the way our car moves, including torque and steering response, differential, transmission, and engine settings. Unreal comes with great tools that help alot here.


Adding a keybind to one of Unreal's built-in AI functions in a Blueprint.

Prototyping in Unreal is extremely fast thanks to Blueprints, it's node-based visual scripting environment. Blueprints are 10x slower than C++, but it's easy to detect hotspots using Unreal's fantastic GPU and CPU profilers.


Unreal CPU profiler (left) and GPU profiler (right)
Press `` to open the Unreal Console and one of the dev commands.

We ship the Deepdrive sim as an Unreal development build, meaning you can call any of built-in Unreal dev commands or call your own C++ from the Unreal console within the game without needing to use the Unreal Editor.

We also use Substance and its Unreal integration for many of our materials, a key tool for easily creating extremely realistic surfaces.


Visual Studio debugging. CLion on Linux is also supported.

Unreal's debugging workflow allows you to easily break anywhere into engine code and hot-reload C++ in your project.

Overall, we've been very happy with Unreal Engine, a AAA game development platform with accessible and modifiable source, a promising long-term financial outlook due to its immensely popular games, being free for non-commercial use, and a solid start at becoming the engine of choice for self-driving simulation. We look forward to contributing to the project and openly testing all self-driving AI with it.

A final note

Creating fully autonomous cars will mean developing AI that has a grounded understanding of physics, our 3D world, and how to interact, predict, and cooperate with humans in a robust way. This makes it critically important that we develop self-driving in a way that is sensitive to AI in general and the sweeping effects it will have, where safety is of top importance. Deepdrive and our simulation platform are therefore about more than testing self-driving cars. It's about openly developing artificial intelligence optimized for safety before AI gets to a point where the time for setting a precedant and building responsible practices, community, and infrastructure has passed.
