Rebuilding Deepdrive on Unreal Engine
April 12, 2018

TLDR; Deepdrive has been rebuilt without GTA using Unreal Engine. Check it out!

Background - or skip to Unreal stuff

A little over a year ago, I worked with OpenAI to release an integration for GTAV into the Universe platform. The goal was to make one of the most complex video games available to researchers to experiment with methods like deep reinforcement learning for self-driving.

However, it turned out that TakeTwo was not as receptive to people using GTAV for this purpose as I had hoped.

Deepdrive was taken down early 2017 due to legal issues around using GTAV. Image from Deepdrive Universe blog post.

I apologize for not having gotten TakeTwo's permission to use GTA. I know that many of you had spent time with Deepdrive 1.0 and Deepdrive Universe, and am deeply sorry for not being able to continue to support your efforts. I hope that rebuilding the project on Unreal will be able to restore what you found valuable in the GTA version, and eventually do much more.

Before the Universe integration launched, I had started work at Uber's Advanced Technologies Group on their perception team. Deepdrive caught their eye as relevant experience applying deep learning to self-driving. It was also an opportunity to incorporate real world concerns back into Deepdrive, something I was grateful that Uber was supportive of.


Levine's Fall 2017 Deep RL course slides

One thing I noticed early on there was that I had solved some of the same problems in simulation as had been solved for real-world cars, but in a much different way. For example, in imitation learning, you often need to reduce sampling error when the training and test distributions are not IID.


Bojarski et al. ‘16, NVIDIA

In real cars this is dealt with, by "pretending" as if the left or right camera is in the center of the car and generating a corresponding corrective steering action to get back to the center. Viewpoint transformation then can interpolate between cameras to generate additional corrective labels under the assumption of an infinite horizon - i.e. flat roads.

In simulation, we can (safely!) vary the trajectory of the car and see what an oracle path-following AI does to correct its trajectory.

Example of randomly varying the trajectory to reduce sampling error. Frames captured after random actions are discarded, so that the agent is only trained on the course corrective behavior.

So it’d be great to use simulated training to improve real-world performance as we can get much more accurate corrective actions. More broadly, we can just try a lot more and do so much more safely. One of the amazing potential opportunities in simulation is to learn to create a 3D model from 2D images, then use GANs to adapt that mapping to the real-world.

Using motion capture sensors for training a robot to act using just a camera. Levine, Finn, et al. 16

Excitingly, when we worked on verifying this idea at Uber - that agents can transfer between real and virtual environments, we found that neural networks trained on real-world driving were able to drive in simulated environments surprisingly well. e.g. The car would steer in the correct direction and stay in the lane, although it would swerve more than it did on a real road. This meant going the other direction was promising (virtual->real), and something I am still very bullish about trying.

Over the course of a year at Uber, I was able to familiarize myself more with the problems real autonomous vehicles by digging into the fundamental reasons for our disengagements and riding along in test cars. Doing so gave me a deep appreciation for the difficultly of the problem and caused me to become increasingly excited about what an open self-driving simulator could to help solve make self-driving happen.

So in November, I left to do just that and began work on Deepdrive 2.0.

The move to Unreal

Some of the popular games made in Unreal - Gears 4, PUBG, and Fortnite from left to right. PUBG and Fortnite are currently the most popular online games with 2M and 3M concurrent users respectively (that's close to the number of concurrent users on Facebook!).

The first step in developing Deepdrive 2.0 was choosing an engine. Of the many we evaluated, Unreal not only checked the boxes, but felt best to us. From a feature perspective Unreal is the only free, shared source, AAA engine available. Having the source is critical as we're going to need to optimize 8+ camera rendering, possibly swap out the physics engine, and run faster than real-time.

Unreal's self-driving ecosystem

Besides Deepdrive, open source self-driving simulation projects like AirSim and Carla are also using Unreal. It’s my hope these sims can reuse components and cooperate. We have already worked with the Carla team on some synchronization functionality and it’s very exciting to see the growing community around open self-driving. In addition, the self-driving simulators at NVIDIA, Cruise, and Zoox are all built on Unreal, and there are likely more who've not yet gone public about it.

Self-driving simulators built in Unreal

You may be wondering why we created our own open source sim in Unreal with the other options available. While we actually were not aware of Carla, or that AirSim was doing more than drones, we found the following out after the fact:

  1. Our frame rates are higher while capturing several cameras, due to use of shared memory rather than sockets and asynchronous transfer.
  2. Our road surface is not flat, but hilly, curvy, and varies in width
  3. Our assets - the map, cars, lighting, etc… are free, come with the project, and modifiable in Unreal.

I think it’s great, however, to have a variety of simulators available. A cross-simulator benchmark would be extremely helpful for helping test agents ability to transfer between environments before transferring into the real-world. If you’re interested in working on this, let me know.

Developing in Unreal

You don't need to develop in Unreal to use Deepdrive. You just need to write Python. However, I think it's interesting and sometimes very helpful to be able to modify the environment for your experiments.

Visual scripting


Adding a keybind to one of Unreal's built-in AI functions in a Blueprint.

Prototyping in Unreal is extremely fast thanks to Blueprints, it's node-based scripting environment. Blueprints are 10x slower at runtime than C++, but it's easy to detect hotspots with Unreal's GPU and CPU profilers and reimplement slow Blueprint code in C++.


Unreal CPU profiler and GPU profiler

Unreal console

Press `` in Deepdrive to open the Unreal Console and one of the dev commands.

We ship our sim as an Unreal development build, meaning you can call any of built-in Unreal dev commands straight from the game. You can also call your own C++ the same way with custom commands.

Substance

We use Substance and its Unreal integration for many of our materials, a key tool for easily creating extremely realistic surfaces.

Debugging workflow


Visual Studio debugging. CLion on Linux is also supported.

Unreal's debugging workflow allows you to easily break anywhere into engine code or your own code, then hot-reload C++ in your project.

Overall, we've been very happy with Unreal Engine, a AAA game development platform with accessible and modifiable source, and a solid start at becoming the engine of choice for self-driving simulation. We look forward to allowing many more people to use it for improving the state of the art in self-driving.

A final note

Creating fully autonomous cars will mean developing AI that has a grounded understanding of physics, our 3D world, and how to interact, predict, and cooperate with humans in a robust way. Simulation allows us to do this in a way that is sensitive sweeping effects AI will have, where ensuring safety, transparency, and broad participation are of utmost importance. Deepdrive is about more than testing self-driving cars. It's about openly developing AI optimized for safety before AI gets to a point where the time for setting a precedent and building responsible practices, community, and infrastructure has passed. It's also important to realize how unique self-driving is in that privacy and secrecy concerns are far fewer than other large AI applications (i.e. search and social), and that involving more people is not only good for long-term safety democratization of AI, but is also extremely helpful in making the technology safer in the short-term.
