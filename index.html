DeepDrive

open platform for self-driving car AI

Motivation

Self-driving cars are poised to become the most impactful technology of the 21st century. Yet, to work on them, you must be in one of the few companies who have taken on the task. Even then, you would be working in relative isolation from other groups doing exactly the same thing. This does not have to be the case. Linux, PostGres, and ROS are just a few examples of a different approach -- where companies, researchers, and individuals work together on foundational technology to create a common platform for bringing better products to market. DeepDrive aims to be that platform for self-driving cars powered by deep learning. By combining highly realistic simulations with hooks for deep learning, DeepDrive gives anyone with a computer the opportunity to build better self-driving cars.

The current generation of self-driving cars struggle in an urban setting at speeds above 25mph, requiring people to be ever-ready at the wheel. This problem is aggravated by the high cost of driving, high risk of accidents, and the resultant limitation on the ability to test new approaches. Modern video-games like GTAV, however, present a world where you can test self-driving car AI’s in large complex urban areas with realistic roads, weather, pedestrians, cyclists, and vehicles with zero cost or risk. Additionally using deep learning in this environment offers the opportunity to learn the nuanced driving behaviors that we expect self-driving cars will have.



The incredibly high fidelity and vast open world provided by GTAV also presents the most complex virtual RL environment for testing interactive AI to date. This allows a new level of testing for safety in AI as existing environments don’t offer the same opportunities for reward hacking, distributional shift, and negative side effects. Finally, developing self-driving cars out in the open provides a level of transparency not usually seen in AI applications and in an area where it is crucial to have visibility into both the safety and correctness of the system.
